Let
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:m:20}
\begin{aligned}
b_i &= b_{ij} e_j \\
w_i &= w_{ij} e_j \\
w_{ij} &= \sigma_j b_{ij}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Given

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:m:40}
\begin{aligned}
x = x^i b_i
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

compute

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:m:60}
\begin{aligned}
x = u^i w_i
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:m:80}
\begin{aligned}
x^i b_i &= u^j w_j \\
x^i b_{ik} e_k &= u^j \sigma_c b_{jc} e_c \\
x^i b_{ik} e_k \cdot e_d &= u^j \sigma_c b_{jc} e_c \cdot e_d \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This leaves us with \(N\) equations (one for each \(e_d\)), for \(M\) unknowns \(u^j\)

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:m:100}
\begin{aligned}
x^i b_{id} &= u^j \sigma_d b_{jd} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

You know there is a unique solution for this provided the matrix \(\sigma_d\) is not degenerate (ie: no zeros), so I do not see any choice but to do the transpose multiplication and invert for \(u^j\)
